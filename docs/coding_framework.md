# Coding framework

The coding framework was created to direct interview questions and thematic analysis.

| Theme | Code | Description | Justification |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Economic | Livelihood needs | Any description of income stability, household dependence, and long-term well-being which shape spatial fishing choices, including trade-offs between economic gain, effort, and sustaining livelihoods under changing conditions. | Fishers’ spatial and behavioral choices are influenced by their priorities and trade off different forms of well-being when deciding how to cope and adapt to changing fisheries conditions (Andrews et al., 2021; Ran et al., 2014; Salas and Gaertner, 2004). |
| Economic | Market dynamics | Any mention of how fish prices change with demand, export needs, festivals, ban seasons, middlemen control, or the number of boats landing fish. Includes timing trips to earn better prices. | Higher fish prices shape where fishers go. When markets reward valuable species, fishers travel farther or take greater risks to reach profitable grounds and time trips to periods of better sales (Girardin et al., 2016; Tsikliras and Polymeros, 2014; Watson et al., 2022). |
| Economic | Running costs | Any reference to rising fishing costs influencing decisions, including fuel, chemical, food, or trip expenses and high-cost long-distance trips. | Fishing costs shape spatial decisions as fishers weigh the expense of distant grounds against expected gains and adjust target combinations when travel becomes expensive (Andersen et al., 2012). |
| Environmental | Seasonality & Monsoon | Any description of moving to areas where bans are not active in a particular season, species-specific seasons, monsoon, seasonal availability/visibility of fish, lunar cycles, and temperature. | Seasonal patterns create predictable cycles in fish availability, fishing access, and operational safety, helping explain recurring peaks in fishing effort and spillover effects when areas are seasonally restricted (Birkenbach et al., 2020; Ni and Sandal, 2019; Robinson et al., 2014). |
| Environmental | Target strategies | Any reference to shifts in target species and fishing grounds, and how boat size, engine power, or gear type limit or enable fishing in deeper, distant, or rough areas. | Fishers move to areas where target species are most available and switch species to keep income steady. Vessel size and gear set limits on depth and distance, creating predictable spatial patterns (Collins et al., 2021; Pavlowich & Kapuscinski, 2017; Poos et al., 2010; Monroy et al., 2010). |
| Environmental | Environmental cues | Any reference to fishers following environmental signs such as birds, dolphins, water colour, currents, or fronts, or choosing grounds shaped by reefs, ridges, or seamounts. | Environmental cues reduce search time and increase expected catch. Ocean features and underwater structures concentrate prey and predators, creating hotspots that repeatedly draw fishers (Baird and Webster, 2020; Kerry et al., 2022; Funk et al., 2019; Watson et al., 2018). |
| Social | Past experiences | References to returning to familiar, named, or historically successful fishing grounds based on experience, habit, or mental maps. | Fishers develop regular search patterns based on past experiences and mental maps, helping them learn where to find fish and creating predictable core areas (Lima et al., 2017; Berkström et al., 2019). |
| Social | Perceptions of catch | References to moving farther offshore when local grounds feel empty or catches drop, avoiding crowded areas, or being displaced by competition and LED fishing. | As nearshore catches decline, fishers travel farther to maintain catch. Competition and LED fishing further reduce available fish and push fishers towards less congested zones (Guillen and Maynou, 2016; Solomon and Ahmad, 2016; Poos and Rijnsdorp, 2007; Wilson et al., 2019). |
| Social | Social networks | Any reference to peer communications, in-trip updates, owner-led destination choices, and switching grounds based on information from other fishers. | Social connections help fishers coordinate search efforts and move together toward temporary fishing hotspots (Turner et al., 2014; Joo et al., 2020). |
| Social | Socio-ecological shock | Mentions of how the pandemic, lockdowns, travel restrictions, market or supply-chain disruptions, or fears of infection influenced decisions about where to fish. | COVID-19 and associated mobility/market disruptions altered where and how fishing vessels operated by reducing effort, limiting offshore operations, and redistributing fishing grounds (Russo et al., 2021; He et al., 2021). |
| Technological | Charts/GPS/AIS/Nav apps | References to instrumental navigation and planning: using charts/GPS to read depth and record locations, AIS to see borders/other boats, saving/returning to coordinates, and noting features. | Technology such as satellite-based PFZ advisories and onboard equipment guides fishers toward productive grounds, reducing search time, increasing catch, and shaping fishing locations (Eigaard et al., 2014; Tummala et al., 2008). |
| Regulatory | Spatial regulations | References to how fear of fines, past penalties, and increased patrolling shape where fishers go, including avoidance of restricted waters and concerns about licenses or permits. | Fisher spatial behavior is shaped by trade-offs between gains from illegal intrusion and perceived probability of detection and sanctions. Weak enforcement undermines deterrence (Sutinen and Kuperan, 1999; Arias and Sutton, 2013; Petrossian, 2015). |
